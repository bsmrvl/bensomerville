I sometimes do small session jobs online, recording piano tracks for distant producers I'll probably never meet. It's quite convenient during pandemics, but never the most lucrative, because I set my rates as if I'm a seasoned professional who can knock out the project in half an hour--only to have it take me eight hours because I wasn't good enough for the job to start with.

With the wrong attitude this can get miserable quickly, as you try over and over to play that same pattern which you'll never get, because you haven't worked on the prerequisite technique. Or you keep deliriously hitting the send button with a file attached which you know isn't good enough, praying the client will somehow overlook the flaws and find it perfect. And of course they don't. (actually, some do--accidentally playing their part in the perpetuation of your mediocrity).

But provided the right attitude, these sub-minimum-wage gigs are some of the best teachers. For one thing, they force you to master things that people are actually paying for!
Here's my humble little keyboard studio, where I've spent most of the past three days working on a 10-second loop. If you clicked on the gif up top, that's not even the loop I'm talking about, but let's start there.

That first one is from a song called Sunday Best, which I hadn't heard (and still haven't listened to fully) until a client asked me to record a jazzy, soulful loop in the same style. I listened to a few seconds and said hell yeah, I'm good at that stuff, I'll knock out a few ideas in 30 minutes.

I've already alluded to what really happened...

But! Eventually I stumbled into that "right attitude" mentioned above. I realized that the client wasn't into anything I was coming up with, simply because I wasn't playing like Sunday Best--because I didn't have those skills!

I remembered a method I've always preached but apparently haven't followed too closely: if you want to learn something, copy (and I mean replicate) examples of that something until it starts flowing out of you naturally. 

I've tended to exempt myself from that method because I'm just so good at improvising. But what I realized the other day was--I'm absolutely no good at improvising in this style. 
So I decided to try following my own advice, and it worked miraculously well. It took a very long time, but it worked. I learned the original part and that made it much easier to come up with ideas in the same style. I wrote and recorded a badass loop, the client is thrilled (when he releases his track I will be sure to link!), I'm 30 whole dollars richer, and all to practice something which will serve my playing for the rest of my life! It's not a bad deal.

Just not one that you should ever view as a primary income source. That will only leave you delirious and mediocre. 
That's all! Can't wait to see the state of the world by next week!

-Ben
